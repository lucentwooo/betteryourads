# Airbnb — Ad 2: "Over 2 million 5-star ratings"

**Image:** ./airbnb-ad-2.jpg
**Awareness Stage:** Solution Aware
**How to recognize this stage:** The ad sells trust/proof (2M+ 5-star ratings, Editors' Choice) to someone considering a travel booking platform but not yet committed to one. No destination, no price, no trip type — it's a platform credibility ad, not a listing ad.

## The Angle
Social proof at platform scale. Where an individual listing might have 100 reviews, Airbnb has *2 million* 5-star ratings across the entire platform. The angle: *you can't comparison-shop us because no competitor has this level of distributed trust.* Also slots in App Store "Editors' Choice" for platform-level endorsement.

## The Concept
Three-phone display showing the product's surface area — an Experiences listing (cooking class in Paris), the Homes home screen, and a Trips details page. Headline + dual Editors' Choice badges sit above. Insight: the ad is a *product tour and a trust stack in one frame*. Viewer sees what the app does + gets social proof stamps + sees the 2M rating anchor.

## Copy Breakdown
- **Headline:** "Over 2 million / 5-star ratings" — BFB (Big-Faceless-Benefit) with a numerical anchor. "Over" is the trust hedge (like AG1's "up to"). Two-line break emphasizes the number.
- **Credibility badges:** Two "Editors' Choice" laurels — one with the App Store laurel wreath icon, one with a certificate-style icon. Two different store badges (App Store + Google Play likely) to cover both ecosystems.
- **UI content:** The three phone screens serve as implicit feature tour — Experiences (cooking classes), Services, Homes (search, recently viewed). No explicit feature callouts, just product visibility.
- **Hook mechanism:** Dollar-sized numerical anchor ("2 million") + social proof badges.

## Design Analysis
- **Composition:** Symmetrical top-down layout. Logo centered top, headline below, twin Editors' Choice badges on a line, then three phones side-by-side at the bottom (middle phone slightly elevated as hero, flanking phones trimmed at edges). Creates a "stage" arrangement — hero in center, supporting cast at sides.
- **Typography:** Airbnb's house sans (Circular-adjacent), extra-bold headline in near-black. Two-line headline with tight leading. Badges in small caps italic (Editors' Choice) with laurel framing.
- **Color system:** Warm cream background (~#F2ECE3 — close to AG1's stone), near-black text, Airbnb Rausch pink logo (the only chromatic element), muted UI colors inside the phones (coral kitchen walls, pastel bedrooms). 3-color canvas restraint, but the phone screens add saturation in a contained way.
- **Imagery:** Three phones, each showing a different section of the Airbnb app. The middle phone is the app home (Start your search) — the hero moment. Left phone: Experiences detail. Right phone: a Trip detail for LA. Three phones = breadth of product without a bullet list.
- **Grid / layout:** Centered editorial with symmetric phone array. Classic app-launch / product-marketing layout.
- **Brand register:** Luxury DTC / Premium app. Closest analog: Apple's feature pages, Calm/Headspace premium ads, Revolut's App Store promo shots.

## Why It Converts
1. **2M is the right-sized anchor.** Large enough to be impressive, specific enough to feel real. "100k ratings" would feel small; "10M" would feel suspicious.
2. **Editors' Choice = third-party validation.** App Store curation badges bypass the viewer's "brands say anything" filter. Platform-level endorsement > self-endorsement.
3. **Three phones = breadth of product.** Without listing features, shows the viewer "Airbnb does Homes AND Experiences AND Trips."
4. **Warm cream background = luxury hospitality tone.** Puts the ad in a magazine-ad register rather than a tech-ad register.
5. **"Recently viewed homes" visible in hero phone = retargeting echo.** If you've been searching Airbnb recently, that UI element feels eerily familiar.

## Why It Looks Premium
- Strict symmetric composition with one asymmetric break (middle phone taller).
- Single accent color (Rausch pink logo) against mostly monochrome palette.
- Phones are rendered at photorealistic quality with subtle shadow, not flat.
- No drop-shadows on type, no gradient mesh, no star-burst badges.
- Cream background echoes luxury DTC conventions.

## Creative Director Lens
Solid system-level work. This is what Airbnb pushes at App Store moment / WWDC / feature launches — a credibility ad with real product surface shown. The twin Editors' Choice badges are interesting — mirroring them on either side of a vertical divider is a precise design choice that reads as "endorsed twice" but almost risks looking redundant. A CD might cut to one badge at larger scale. Also: the Experiences phone on the left is cropped aggressively (you lose ~30% of the content), which creates an intentional "there's more" vibe but risks reading as a mistake at small scale. The middle phone's home screen is the correct hero choice — Start your search is the only CTA in the entire ad, and it lives inside the product. Overall this is mid-funnel brand-lift work — not as striking as Ad 1 but does a different job well.

## Media Buyer Lens
Best for **platform trust campaigns and app-install objectives.** Run on cold audiences unfamiliar with Airbnb or on competitor-app users (Booking, VRBO, Hotels.com interest targeting). CPM: $12-20. Expected CTR for app installs: 0.8-1.3%. For booking objective campaigns: 0.5-0.9%. This is a trust-building ad, so pair with Ad 1 (confirmation screen, warm retargeting) or Ad 3 (quick/easy positioning) in a sequenced funnel. Fatigue slower than transactional ads (~3 weeks). Weakness: no destination or offer means conversion rate on click is lower than a listing-specific ad.

## Transferable Patterns
- **"Over [big number] [trust metric]" headline.** Specific number + "over" hedge + trust metric (ratings, users, transactions) = platform-level credibility in one line.
- **Third-party badge pair.** When you have App Store Editors' Choice or similar, run two badges mirrored around a divider — reads as "endorsed across ecosystems."
- **Three-phone product display.** Show 3 screens of your app side-by-side with the hero phone slightly taller. Communicates breadth without feature bullets.
- **Match brand color to logo only.** When your brand color is strong (Rausch pink, ClickUp yellow), limit it to the logo and let the canvas stay neutral.
