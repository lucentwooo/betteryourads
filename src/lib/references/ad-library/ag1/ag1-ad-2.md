# AG1 — Ad 2: Sloane Stephens Testimonial

**Image:** ./ag1-ad-2.jpg
**Awareness Stage:** Solution Aware
**How to recognize this stage:** No feature mention, no offer, no dollar value. Pure social proof from a high-status athlete. This reaches viewers who know they want health/performance support but haven't chosen a brand — the celebrity testimonial is the tiebreaker.

## The Angle
Elite performer social proof. Sloane Stephens (US Open winner) becomes the proxy — if she uses it, it must actually work at the highest performance level. The angle subtly attacks AG1's weakness (it's an expensive powder, easy to dismiss as wellness-influencer bullshit) by pairing it with someone whose performance is measurable.

## The Concept
Overhead hero shot of Sloane mid-serve on a tennis court. Her quote sits in the top-right corner, treated like a magazine caption. No product visible at all. Insight: the best testimonials don't show the product — they show the *person the product helps.* The viewer fills in the association.

## Copy Breakdown
- **Headline (quote):** "AG1 is one of those little things that helps me *feel grounded and perform my best.*" — Story-First framework. Two moves inside one sentence:
  - "one of those little things" = humility / authenticity. She's not saying AG1 changed her life (unbelievable from a pro athlete); she's saying it's one small daily ritual. Way more credible.
  - *italicized "feel grounded and perform my best"* = the emotional payload. Italic weighting marks this as the *actual* testimonial phrase.
- **Attribution block:** "**Sloane S.** / US Open Winner / *Real AG1 Member*" — three-line credibility stack. First name + last initial (intimate, not publicist-written). Title in caps for authority. "Real AG1 Member" italicized — differentiates her from paid ambassadors (even if she is one).
- **Hook mechanism:** Face + quote. Human faces in feed beat product shots 3:1 for stopping power.

## Design Analysis
- **Composition:** Full-bleed overhead sports photography. Sloane occupies the left 60%, quote sits top-right 30% in negative space (sky-of-the-court). Racket extends into the quote zone — intentional overlap that anchors the copy to her.
- **Typography:** Same editorial serif as Ad 1 (Canela/Tiempos-adjacent) for the quote. Clean sans for attribution. Italic treatment on the emotional phrases. White text over the tennis court green — high contrast, zero decoration.
- **Color system:** Forced by the photograph — tennis court green (~#2A6A3E) dominates, Sloane's aqua/turquoise tennis dress is the second color, red/orange racket handle is the accent. Three-color palette emerges naturally from the shot — no graphic overlay color introduced. The ad respects the photo.
- **Imagery:** Overhead action photography (drone-style or high angle). Sloane is mid-serve, racket raised. The framing is editorial sports-photography grade, not stock. She's looking up toward the quote — her gaze line subtly guides the eye to the copy.
- **Grid / layout:** Rule-of-thirds — face/quote occupy the upper-right third intersection point. Classic editorial.
- **Brand register:** Luxury DTC / Editorial sports. Closest analog: Nike "You Can't Stop Us," Lululemon "Be Present" campaigns.

## Why It Converts
1. **No product = no ad smell.** The viewer consumes it as editorial content before realizing it's a brand message.
2. **"Little things" is the linguistic Trojan horse.** Wellness products drown in grandiose claims ("transform your health"). Sloane saying "little thing" is counter-positioning through humility, and it's way more credible.
3. **Italic marks the quotable phrase.** If the viewer only reads one phrase, it's *"feel grounded and perform my best"* — AG1's actual desired positioning.
4. **"Real AG1 Member" kills the "she's paid to say this" objection.** Even if paid, calling her a member implies daily use.
5. **Green-on-green** — brand color and photo color align naturally, which means the ad feels on-brand without any AG1 logo forcing its way into the composition.

## Why It Looks Premium
- No logo in the frame (ultimate brand-confidence move — the quote signature does the job).
- Photography is editorial-grade, not stock or influencer selfie.
- Zero graphic elements — no badges, no CTAs, no product shots.
- Type hierarchy is quote / attribution, that's it.
- White text on photo with no box or shadow — trusts the photo's negative space.

## Creative Director Lens
This is a CD's favorite kind of ad to approve. It respects the photograph, it trusts the viewer to complete the association, and it leverages a real athlete instead of a paid creator. The italic-within-quote treatment is sophisticated — it's how New Yorker profiles pull quotes, not how DTC ads usually work. Minor craft note: the attribution "US Open Winner" is specific and good — "Professional Tennis Player" would be weaker. One concern: running this without the AG1 pouch visible anywhere might confuse cold audiences who don't already know the brand. This is almost certainly a **mid-funnel ad** — it's the reinforcement layer, not the introduction. A CD would want to see it in a sequence with Ad 1 or 3.

## Media Buyer Lens
This is a **brand-lift / mid-funnel creative**, not a direct-response unit. Expect lower CTR (0.4-0.8% across audiences) but higher brand recall and lower long-term CAC because it pre-warms audiences for conversion creatives. Best audiences: tennis/sports fans, lookalikes of existing subscribers, health/wellness interest targeting. CPM: $22-32. Run on reach-optimized campaigns or as the first creative in a sequenced campaign (day 1 = Sloane testimonial, day 3 = Ad 1 offer). The absence of an explicit CTA is a *feature* at this stage — it's building association, not pushing a transaction. Lifespan: 3-4 weeks before fatigue because there's no time-bound hook to decay.

## Transferable Patterns
- **Celebrity/athlete quote, no product in frame.** Trust the viewer to complete the association. The absence of the product is what makes it editorial, not advertorial.
- **Italic-within-quote for emotional emphasis.** Pull the specific phrase you want remembered into italic — most viewers will only read that fragment.
- **"Little thing" humility framing.** For wellness/performance products, understating the benefit is more credible than overstating it.
