# AG1 — Ad 3: "Anna C. Science Teacher" Gut Health UGC

**Image:** ./ag1-ad-3.jpg
**Awareness Stage:** Problem Aware → Solution Aware bridge
**How to recognize this stage:** The testimonial is anchored on a *felt symptom* ("more supported in my gut health") — meaning the viewer already knows they have gut issues. It's not explaining what AG1 does; it's naming a felt problem the viewer already has. The FREE stamp handles the solution-aware closing.

## The Angle
Relatable everyday-person social proof. Where Ad 2 used an elite athlete, this ad swaps to "Anna C, Science Teacher" — someone viewers can project onto. The angle: *"if this normal woman got results, you will too."* AG1 knows their ICP isn't pro athletes, it's 30-50yo women managing their own health, and this creative targets them specifically.

## The Concept
Vertical (9:16) UGC-style photo — a woman mid-drink from a shaker bottle with AG1 inside, shot in natural daylight in what looks like a residential street/park. Overlay: a quote in a floating white card (top), a green attribution label, and a green "Claim Your FREE Welcome Kit" stamp in the bottom right. Insight: UGC/creator-shot photos outperform studio for problem-aware audiences because they look like content from a friend, not from a brand.

## Copy Breakdown
- **Headline (quote):** "It's really amazing how I just feel *more supported in my gut health.*" — Story-First framework. "It's really amazing" is colloquial not advertorial — sounds like someone actually talking. "Just feel" hedges the claim (she's not saying it cures anything, she's saying she *feels* it — unfalsifiable testimonial, legally safer and psychologically more credible). Italicized "more supported in my gut health" is the exact claim AG1 wants to own.
- **Attribution:** "Real AG1 Member / **Anna C.** / *Science Teacher*" — same three-line structure as Ad 2 (Sloane). "Science Teacher" is a deliberate occupation choice — implies rational, educated, not a wellness-influencer mark.
- **Offer stamp:** "Claim Your FREE Welcome Kit" in a green circular badge, tilted slightly — retro/grocery-flyer energy. Deliberately disrupts the editorial tone because the ad needs a conversion mechanism.
- **Footer:** "**Offer Valid For New Subscribers Only" — tiny legal.
- **Hook mechanism:** Shaker bottle close-up (product in action) + relatable face + offer stamp.

## Design Analysis
- **Composition:** 9:16 vertical (Reels/Stories native). Person occupies the lower 65%, with the shaker bottle in front of her face as the product hero. Top 25% is negative space (sky/trees blurred) with the quote card floating. Offer stamp bottom-right third.
- **Typography:** Same editorial serif (Canela/Tiempos-adjacent) for the quote. Attribution sans. Stamp uses sans in white on green. The stamp's typographic choice (all-caps FREE, italic "Claim Your" and "Welcome Kit") mimics grocery-store price flyers — deliberate tonal shift to scream "offer."
- **Color system:** Natural photo palette (green trees, warm stone pavement, her coral/salmon tank top), AG1 green for the quote attribution box and offer stamp. Coral top + AG1 green = complementary contrast that makes the human pop against the product branding.
- **Imagery:** UGC-style / creator-shot portrait. Natural lighting, shallow depth of field (bokeh background), authentic composition (she's not posing for the camera — she's drinking). The shaker bottle is specifically foregrounded to associate the green liquid with the quote.
- **Grid / layout:** Vertical rule-of-thirds with elements floating on the image. Feels like a story/reel screenshot, not a designed graphic.
- **Brand register:** Wellness DTC with creator/UGC crossover. Closest analog: Ritual, Olipop, Athletic Greens' creator partnership ads.

## Why It Converts
1. **"Science Teacher" > "Verified Buyer."** Gives the testimonial a specific, rational-sounding identity. Viewers trust teachers more than random reviewers.
2. **Gut health is the #1 felt symptom in AG1's ICP.** Calling it out explicitly = immediate ICP recognition.
3. **UGC framing bypasses ad-blindness.** The photo looks like a friend's story post, which means the first 0.5 seconds of scroll don't register it as advertising.
4. **"Feel supported" is a legally-safe claim.** No FDA issues — it's a feeling, not a health outcome.
5. **FREE stamp closes the loop.** Without the stamp this is a brand-lift ad. With the stamp it's a conversion ad. The retro-flyer design of the stamp is intentional — it's visually different from the editorial quote, which makes it function as a separate "call to action" layer.

## Why It Looks Premium
- Editorial serif quote preserves brand register even on UGC-style photo.
- Green attribution box and green offer stamp tie the ad to AG1's color system.
- The quote card has a subtle shadow but feels "taped onto" the photo rather than overlaid — Instagram sticker aesthetic done well.
- Photo is high-quality (not phone-quality) despite the UGC vibe.
- No logo in the hero frame — attribution box carries brand.

## Creative Director Lens
This is a **creator-economy crossover ad** — it walks the line between editorial testimonial (Ad 2) and influencer UGC. The shaker bottle product placement is the hero move — the green liquid is visible, drinkable, and tied to the quote in one glance. Minor critique: the offer stamp, while effective, feels tonally inconsistent with the editorial quote treatment above — it's almost like two different brands collided. A CD might push to either (a) redesign the stamp in the brand's editorial sans to match, or (b) lean fully into the stamp's retro-flyer energy and make the whole ad more playful. Pick a lane. That said, the current version probably tests best for conversion precisely because of that tension. Good ad, imperfect taste.

## Media Buyer Lens
This is the **cold-acquisition Reels unit** for AG1. Vertical format + creator-style photo + explicit offer = engineered for Reels/Stories placement. CPM on Reels for wellness: $18-30. Expected CTR: 1.5-2.5% on broad cold audiences (gut health, wellness, women 35-55). ROAS on first-month subscription: 1.8-2.8x. This ad will fatigue faster than Ad 2 (~8-10 days) because the offer stamp is the stopping element and it's one-and-done. Best run with 2-3 variants rotating (different creator faces, same structure). Weakness: on Facebook desktop feed, the vertical format crops awkwardly — make a 1:1 cut for feed placements.

## Transferable Patterns
- **UGC-style photo + editorial-serif quote overlay.** Contrast between casual photo and refined type reads as "authentic testimonial from real user, brand-vetted." Higher trust than pure creator UGC.
- **Occupation-based attribution.** "Science Teacher" beats "Verified Customer" because it gives the testifier a specific, relatable identity.
- **Product-in-hand / drinking shot.** For consumable products, showing the moment of consumption beats showing the packaging.
- **Retro-flyer offer stamp as conversion layer.** When your ad is editorial/UGC, add an explicit graphic CTA layer (stamp, badge, sticker) — don't let the testimonial do all the work.
