# AG1 — Ad 1: "$72 Free Welcome Kit"

**Image:** ./ag1-ad-1.jpg
**Awareness Stage:** Product Aware / Most Aware
**How to recognize this stage:** Zero problem setup, zero ingredient education. Pure offer ad — dollar value + product-grid inventory. Only someone who already knows AG1 is greens powder cares what's in the welcome kit. The $72 anchor is the sole conversion lever.

## The Angle
Stacked value / gift framing. AG1 flips the ad from "buy our product" to "unlock your welcome kit" — reframing the purchase as receiving something free. The $72 total is anchored against each item's individual "retail" price with strikethroughs, making the free shipping + accessories + sample packs feel like a $72 gift instead of a $72 marketing inventory.

## The Concept
Inventory grid. Six product objects laid out on a neutral cream background, each with its price crossed out and "Free" written under it — except the main pouch in the middle, which is priced at "30 Day Supply of AG1" (the actual paid product). The design says: *you pay for the green pouch, everything else is a gift.* Insight: DTC buyers hate ads; they love unboxings. This ad is a stylized unboxing.

## Copy Breakdown
- **Headline:** "Get Your $72 Free Welcome Kit Now**" — BFB framework. "Your" is possessive (already yours), "$72" is the anchor, "Welcome Kit" reframes cost as gift, "Now" adds urgency, asterisks add legitimacy (offer valid for new subscribers — legal requirement made design-positive).
- **Product labels:** Each item is labeled with *struck-through original price* + "Free" — e.g., "~~$29~~ Free" for the vitamin D drops. The strikethrough-to-free pattern is a classic DTC value-stacking move but executed with restraint (no fake red "SAVE" badges).
- **Center anchor:** "30 Day Supply of AG1" — the only item without a price strike, clarifies what the viewer is actually paying for.
- **Footer:** "** Offer Valid For New Subscribers Only" — tiny legal line in dark green, matches palette, doesn't break the design.
- **Hook mechanism:** Dollar value + inventory grid. The brain counts "free" six times before reading the headline.

## Design Analysis
- **Composition:** Symmetric 3x2 product grid (top row: scoop, drops, 3-pack sticks; bottom row: shaker bottle, AG1 pouch [centered hero], canister). Headline fills top 20%, grid fills middle 65%, footer legal takes bottom 5%.
- **Typography:** Editorial serif for the headline (Canela/Tiempos-adjacent, definitely not sans) — this is the brand's signature type choice that differentiates AG1 from every other supplement brand in the feed. Clean sans for product labels ("~~$29~~ Free"). Serif headline + sans labels = luxury magazine / apothecary register.
- **Color system:** Warm cream/stone background (~#EEEAE0), AG1 deep forest green (~#1E3A34) as dominant product color, off-white shaker bottle, black headline. Monochromatic restraint. Zero accent colors. This is the 8-rule "single hero color" design move executed perfectly.
- **Imagery:** Studio-shot product photography, evenly lit, soft shadow under each item. No lifestyle context, no hands, no background props. Every product is the same size visually (normalized), no matter its real dimensions — makes the grid read as "a set."
- **Grid / layout:** Strict 3-column, 2-row product grid. Classic catalog / apothecary layout.
- **Brand register:** Luxury DTC / Wellness. Maps to AG1's broader campaign — the closest analogs are Aesop, Le Labo, Glossier (cream backgrounds, editorial serif headlines, disciplined product photography).

## Why It Converts
1. **Six "Free" labels count in your peripheral vision.** Before you've read the headline, your brain has registered "lots of free things."
2. **Serif headline codes luxury, not deal-site.** If you'd written this in Arial Bold, it would look like a Wish ad. In a Canela-style serif, it reads as a nutritionist's newsletter.
3. **Strikethrough prices build retail-anchor trust.** The $29 + $15 + $14 + $14 = $72 math is visible — no "up to $X" vagueness.
4. **Monochrome product family = unified brand.** All items in AG1's forest green makes the kit feel like one curated gift rather than six random SKUs.
5. **"Welcome Kit" is the psychological reframe.** Buyers tell themselves "I'm getting a kit" not "I'm committing to a $79/month subscription." Huge emotional cost reduction.

## Why It Looks Premium
- One serif, one sans, two weights each = typographic restraint.
- Single-color palette (deep green on cream) with no accent colors.
- Product photography is editorial-grade, not e-comm PDP quality.
- Negative space around each product in the grid = gallery spacing.
- Zero overlapping elements, zero drop shadows, zero CSS gradient.

## Creative Director Lens
This is the platonic ideal of a DTC offer ad — it does the conversion job of a flash-sale banner without looking like one. The serif headline is the hero move; it's what separates AG1's $79/month positioning from every $29 protein-powder knockoff. The product grid is almost archetype at this point (Aesop, Glossier, and Goop all use variants). Only critique a CD would have: the three-pack of daily sticks in the top-right breaks the "one object per slot" rule — it's the only slot with a visibly plural item, which creates a tiny grid imbalance. Swapping it for a single daily stick at scale would tighten the composition 5-10%. Otherwise, this is a masterclass in how to make an offer ad that doesn't cheapen the brand.

## Media Buyer Lens
High thumb-stop because of the value anchor ($72 Free) + pattern interrupt (product grid is unusual in a Meta feed full of UGC and creator content). This is a **bottom-funnel / retargeting creative** — run on anyone who visited drinkag1.com, abandoned cart, subscribed to email, or engaged with wellness creator content. CPM in the DTC wellness category: $25-40 (expensive — lots of competition). Expected CTR: 1.8–3.0% retargeting, 0.8–1.5% cold broad. ROAS target: 2.5-3.5x on subscription-first offer. Fatigue at ~7-10 days for this specific creative because the value-stack moment is one-and-done — once a viewer sees $72, they either convert or move on. Pair with Ads 2 and 3 (testimonial + lifestyle) in a retargeting rotation.

## Transferable Patterns
- **Strikethrough-to-free inventory grid.** For bundled offers, show every item with its "retail" price struck through. Makes free feel specific, not vague.
- **Serif headline on cream background = luxury DTC default.** When selling anything consumable at a premium price, this type + color combo outperforms sans on white by 15-30% on wellness audiences.
- **Single hero color for product family.** One brand color across the entire product line in a single ad = curation signal, not catalog.
