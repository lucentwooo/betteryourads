# Canonical Patterns from the Reference Ad Library

Distilled from 18 ads across 6 brands (Notion, AG1, Airbnb, Duolingo, Shopify, Kalshi). These patterns recurred in 2+ brands and are reliable enough to build into the `ad-creative-generator` skill. Single-brand patterns are noted as such.

---

## A. Copy Patterns

### A1. Dollar-anchored headline for product-aware audiences
**Seen in:** Notion (Ads 1, 3, 4, 6), AG1 (Ad 1), Kalshi (Ad 1 — asymmetric $100 → $1,516)
**Rule:** When the viewer already knows your product, lead with a dollar figure, not a benefit. "$12,000 of Free Notion" / "$72 Free Welcome Kit" / "$100 into $1,516."
**Why it works:** Product-aware viewers have heard every feature claim. A number interrupts the scroll because the brain processes quantities before concepts.
**Formula:** "[Verb] [Specific $ amount] [of/in/Free] [Product]"
**Don't do it for:** Solution-aware or problem-aware stages — numbers feel transactional before the viewer is ready.

### A2. Hedged trust words around big numbers
**Seen in:** Notion ("Get up to $12,000"), Airbnb ("Over 2 million 5-star ratings"), AG1 ("$72 Free" but with itemized math).
**Rule:** Always hedge a big-number claim with "Up to," "Over," or itemized math. Unhedged numbers read as clickbait.
**Formula:** "Up to [$X]" / "Over [N] [metric]"

### A3. Verb-rhythm parallelism for solution-aware
**Seen in:** Notion ("Organize everything. / Accomplish anything.", "Dream it. Map it. Build it. Share it."), Airbnb ("Quick. Easy. Airbnb.")
**Rule:** 2-4 short parallel verbs/adjectives, each ending in a period. No "and." The period forces rhythm.
**Formula:** "[Verb] [it/noun]. [Verb] [it/noun]. [Kicker]."

### A4. Italic-within-quote for emotional emphasis
**Seen in:** AG1 (Ads 2 and 3), Notion (Ad 2 subhead — "meets AI efficiency")
**Rule:** When writing a testimonial quote, italicize the single phrase that carries the desired brand association. Most readers only remember the italic fragment.
**Example:** "AG1 is one of those little things that helps me *feel grounded and perform my best.*"

### A5. Occupation-based attribution, not "Verified Buyer"
**Seen in:** AG1 ("US Open Winner," "Science Teacher"), Shopify ("Founder - Eastside Golf")
**Rule:** Give testimonial sources a specific, relatable (or aspirational) identity. "Science Teacher" > "Verified Customer." "Founder - Eastside Golf" > "Happy Shopify user."

### A6. Cultural question as headline, no brand mention
**Seen in:** Kalshi (Ad 1)
**Rule (single-brand):** Use a real, current cultural question as the headline with zero brand name in the copy. Ad reads as editorial content, not marketing.
**Formula:** "Will [specific person/event] [specific outcome]?"

### A7. "Turn your [X] into your [Y]" transformation template
**Seen in:** Shopify (Ad 3)
**Rule:** Name the gap between viewer's current state and aspirational state in one verb. Works for problem-aware stages.
**Formula:** "Turn your [current state / passion / hobby] into your [desired state / business / career]"

### A8. Radical simplicity for non-native English audiences
**Seen in:** Duolingo (Ad 2)
**Rule (single-brand):** Lowercase, 2-3 word headlines using only basic English vocab when targeting ESL audiences. Form signals audience empathy.

### A9. CTA-as-objection-handler
**Seen in:** Notion ("Free Trial with AI Access," "Free 3 Month Trial with Full AI Access"), AG1 ("Claim Your FREE Welcome Kit")
**Rule:** Put the #1 customer objection (pricing, AI lockout, commitment length) INTO the CTA button text itself. Don't save it for the landing page.

### A10. Dual-CTA ladder (low + high commitment)
**Seen in:** Notion (Ads 4, 6 — "Get Started Free" top + "Free 3 Month Trial" middle)
**Rule:** Two CTAs at different commitment levels let a single ad serve two buyer stages. Soft CTA top-right (low commitment), primary CTA below (high commitment).

## B. Design Patterns

### B1. Proprietary illustration > stock or AI imagery
**Seen in:** Notion (mascot + editorial line art), Duolingo (Duo the owl), Kalshi (line-art portrait)
**Rule:** Even simple line illustrations, when drawn in a consistent house style, build more brand equity than photorealism or stock. Viewers recognize the style before the logo.

### B2. Editorial serif headline + cream background = luxury DTC default
**Seen in:** AG1 (all 3 ads), Airbnb (Ads 2, 3 — warm cream canvas with sans, serif-adjacent)
**Rule:** For premium consumer products ($40+ monthly subscription, or $100+ retail), use editorial serif (Canela/Tiempos/Söhne Breit-adjacent) on off-white/cream (~#F2ECE3 to ~#EEEAE0) backgrounds. Single brand-color accent in logo only.

### B3. Card-in-frame / ad-as-product-window
**Seen in:** Notion (Ads 4, 6 — yellow and blue variants)
**Rule:** Frame the ad inside a rounded card that mimics a browser window, mobile app surface, or Notion-style page. Ad IS the product, not about the product. Include visible UI chrome (window buttons, address bar, app header).

### B4. Ultra-restrained color palette (3 colors max, one accent)
**Seen in:** Notion (all 6), AG1 (all 3), Airbnb (all 3), Duolingo (all ads), Kalshi (Ad 1), Shopify (Ads 1-3)
**Rule:** Every ad in this library uses a maximum of 3 colors + minor accents. No color competes with the focal point. The accent color appears in exactly one place: the logo, the CTA, or a single highlighted word.

### B5. Surgical word-coloring for offer highlights
**Seen in:** Notion (Ads 4, 6 — blue "$12,000" + red "free" while rest is black)
**Rule:** Color only the 2-3 words that carry the offer. Leave the rest of the headline near-black. Colored words become magnets the eye returns to.

### B6. Real product UI screenshots > feature illustrations
**Seen in:** Notion (Ads 4, 6 — stacked Daily Report + Engineering Tasks + Launch Checklist), Airbnb (all ads show real UI), Duolingo (Ad 1 shows Courses screen)
**Rule:** Use actual screenshots of your product UI, even at thumbnail scale. Fake mockups, icon grids, and feature illustrations consistently underperform real screens for SaaS/app brands.

### B7. Logo-off or logo-tiny as confidence move
**Seen in:** Airbnb (Ad 1 — no logo in frame), Kalshi (Ad 1 — tiny logo top), AG1 (Ad 2 — no logo, attribution carries brand), Shopify (Ad 2 — bottom corner)
**Rule:** If your brand or product UI is recognizable, shrink or omit the logo. A small or absent logo signals confidence; a giant logo signals insecurity.

### B8. Type sandwich on full-bleed portrait for Reels
**Seen in:** Shopify (Ad 1)
**Rule (single-brand, but transferable):** For 9:16 vertical Reels/Stories, split the headline into top and bottom lines with a full-bleed portrait photo sandwiched between. Uses the entire vertical canvas.

### B9. Mascot peeking into product UI
**Seen in:** Duolingo (Ad 1 — Duo peeks at Courses list), Notion (Ad 5 — mascot + integration ring)
**Rule:** Place a proprietary mascot/character adjacent to (not inside) a product UI card, with the mascot's gaze pointing toward the product. Creates an implicit narrative: the brand is watching over your experience.

### B10. Ultra-wide negative space (>50% of canvas)
**Seen in:** Notion (Ad 2), Airbnb (all 3), AG1 (all 3), Duolingo (Ad 2), Kalshi (Ad 1)
**Rule:** At least half the canvas is empty. Restraint signals confidence. Busy ads signal insecurity.

### B11. Warm vs. cool canvas for audience segmentation
**Seen in:** Notion (yellow Ad 4 vs. blue Ad 6), Airbnb (warm cream throughout), Duolingo (saturated blue)
**Rule:** Warm backgrounds (yellow/orange/cream) target creators, young audiences, Instagram, DTC lifestyle. Cool backgrounds (blue/gray/white) target enterprise, B2B, LinkedIn, utility apps. Run color variants of the same ad to A/B across audiences.

### B12. Paper-cutout collage for testimonials/founder stories
**Seen in:** Shopify (Ad 2 — Olajuwon + Eastside Golf collage), Shopify (Ad 3 — passion archetypes)
**Rule (single-brand, transferable):** Loose collage of 3-5 images at slight angles (±5 degrees) with subtle shadow gives "founder moodboard" authenticity. Avoids over-designed ad feel.

### B13. Centered editorial poster structure
**Seen in:** Notion (Ads 1-3), AG1 (Ads 1, 2), Kalshi (Ad 1), Duolingo (Ad 2)
**Rule:** Logo top-center, headline center, visual/payoff bottom-center. Strict vertical symmetry. No asymmetric composition tricks. Gravitas through restraint.

### B14. Hand-painted accent on flat layout (humanizer)
**Seen in:** Notion (Ad 3 — green highlighter marker behind "Your way"), AG1 (Ad 3 — tilted FREE stamp)
**Rule:** Include exactly ONE hand-drawn, imperfect, or analog-texture element in an otherwise clean flat layout. Humanizes the ad without breaking the design system.

### B15. Single-color logo on matching-color background
**Seen in:** AG1 (green products on cream), Duolingo (green owl on blue), Notion (blue CTA on yellow/white)
**Rule:** When your brand color dominates the product family, let the canvas be a neutral foil. Don't fight brand color with another saturated color.

## C. Concept/Angle Patterns

### C1. The product UI IS the ad
**Seen in:** Airbnb (Ad 1 — booking confirmation as the whole ad), Notion (Ads 4, 6 — ad is a Notion card)
**Rule:** For digital products, the single strongest creative move is making the ad LOOK like the product — not show the product, but *be* the product's surface. Removes the ad-smell.

### C2. Show the customer's work, not your product (platform ads)
**Seen in:** Shopify (Ad 2 — Eastside Golf merchandise, not Shopify UI)
**Rule:** For platform/tool brands, the most compelling visual is the customer's creation — your product becomes the empty space behind their success.

### C3. "Right way" prescriptive positioning
**Seen in:** Shopify (Ad 2 — "If you're doing it the right way, you're on Shopify")
**Rule (single-brand, transferable):** Prescriptive language ("the right way," "how it should be done") from an authority figure implicitly positions competitors as wrong. Use only when you've earned category authority.

### C4. Cast the audience, don't show talent
**Seen in:** Shopify (Ad 1 — real-looking creator founders), AG1 (Ad 3 — everyday woman)
**Rule:** For creator/founder/lifestyle brands, cast people who LOOK like your ICP — not polished models, not obvious influencers. Casting is the conversion tool.

### C5. News-cycle reactive templating
**Seen in:** Kalshi (Ad 1 — Rory/Augusta)
**Rule (single-brand, high value):** Build a design template where the headline + illustration swap weekly based on trending cultural events. One layout = infinite fresh creatives.

### C6. Dual-register ad: solution-aware headline + product-aware offer
**Seen in:** Notion (Ad 3 — "Dream it. Map it..." + $12K offer), Notion (Ad 6 — "Organize Everything..." + trial offer)
**Rule:** Combine a solution-aware creative headline with a product-aware offer below. Same creative serves both stages.

### C7. Unification/equation layout for multi-tool integration
**Seen in:** Notion (Ad 5 — mascot + logo ring)
**Rule (single-brand, transferable):** For tools that integrate with competitors, show them as a visual equation: [Your brand] + [competitor logos] = [outcome]. Normalize all logos into same-size circles to kill visual competition.

### C8. Low-probability / high-reward asymmetric math
**Seen in:** Kalshi (Ad 1 — 6.6% chance / $100 → $1,516)
**Rule (single-brand):** For products with asymmetric outcomes (trading, lottery-like, insurance, upside plays), present the probability and payoff side-by-side. The viewer does the math and creates their own pitch.

## D. Awareness-Stage → Pattern Mapping

| Stage | Preferred patterns | Avoid |
|-------|-------------------|-------|
| **Unaware** | C5 (news-cycle), C4 (cast-the-audience), B2 (editorial serif) | Feature lists, pricing, CTAs |
| **Problem Aware** | A7 (transformation template), A8 (radical simplicity for ESL), C4 (casting), B12 (founder collage) | Dollar anchors, feature callouts |
| **Solution Aware** | A3 (verb rhythm), A4 (italic testimonial), A5 (occupation attribution), B2 (editorial serif), C1 (product-UI-as-ad) | Urgent CTAs, "BUY NOW" |
| **Product Aware** | A1 (dollar anchor), A2 (hedged numbers), A9 (CTA-as-objection-handler), B3 (card-in-frame), B5 (surgical word color), B6 (real UI screenshots) | Long brand stories, category education |
| **Most Aware** | A1 (dollar anchor), A9 (CTA-as-objection), A10 (dual CTA), C1 (product-UI-as-ad for retargeting) | First-impression elements, over-explanation |

## F. Anti-patterns (what these top brands NEVER do)

- **No stock photography anywhere** in these 18 ads (custom illustration or custom photography only)
- **No "ACT NOW," "LIMITED TIME,"** or urgency bark copy — all offer urgency is baked into the specific number/deadline, not shouted
- **No gradient meshes, no glassmorphism, no "AI aesthetic"** — all flat, all disciplined
- **No drop shadows on type** — confidence-killer, always absent in premium ads
- **No multi-stroke text outlines, no text effects** — type is always single-color, single-weight treatments
- **No exclamation marks anywhere** in this library (zero — checked all 18 ads)
- **No emoji in ad copy**
- **No CTAs shouting in all caps** (most CTAs are sentence case or Title Case)
- **No feature bullet lists** — every brand substitutes visual proof (UI screenshots, product grids, logo rings)
