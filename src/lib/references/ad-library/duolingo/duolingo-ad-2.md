# Duolingo — Ad 2: "improve your english"

**Image:** ./duolingo-ad-2.jpg
**Awareness Stage:** Problem Aware
**How to recognize this stage:** Localized, imperative, extremely simple. This is almost certainly an ad targeted at non-English speakers who know they need to improve their English (for work, immigration, travel, school). The lowercase + minimal copy is a deliberate register shift toward international/non-native English audiences. Problem aware because "improve" implies a felt gap.

## The Angle
Radical simplicity for non-native speakers. The copy is lowercase, short, and uses only basic English vocabulary ("improve," "your," "english") — reading level ~B1 or lower. The angle: *we speak to you in the simplest possible English because we know English isn't your first language.* Form IS the message.

## The Concept
Three text elements and Duo. That's the entire ad. "improve your english" headline, "duolingo" below in smaller type, mascot at the bottom, white background. The minimalism forces every element to work 3x harder. Insight: when targeting non-native English audiences, less copy = less cognitive load = more conversions.

## Copy Breakdown
- **Headline:** "improve your english" — Direct Callout, lowercase. Three words, each one is taught in the first 100 words of any English-learning app. No article, no punctuation. The lowercase is a design decision but also a language-register decision — it removes formality / schoolbook energy.
- **Brand signature:** "duolingo" — same typographic treatment as headline, smaller size. Acts as a signature rather than a logo.
- **Hook mechanism:** Mascot face + ultra-simple English text. Non-native English readers scan for "english" (the word they're chasing) and lock in.

## Design Analysis
- **Composition:** Centered vertical. Headline top-center, brand signature below, then a lot of negative space, then Duo standing at the bottom. ~60% of the canvas is white space. Duo is small (~20% of canvas) but anchors the bottom.
- **Typography:** Feather Bold, lowercase. Duolingo green for headline. Lighter gray-green for "duolingo" below. The color contrast between headline green (saturated) and brand green (muted) creates hierarchy without size.
- **Color system:** Pure white background, Duolingo green headline, muted green for brand, Duo in full color. 2-color canvas with Duo adding small color accents (orange beak, orange feet, black eyes). The whole ad feels like a single-color print.
- **Imagery:** Duo mascot standing, small, at the bottom. Slight shadow under his feet grounds him (small gray oval). Mascot is in his "peeking / curious" pose — smiling, side-tilted.
- **Grid / layout:** Centered vertical stack with heavy negative space. Almost poster-minimalist.
- **Brand register:** Youth / Global consumer. Feels closer to Google's homepage minimalism or a children's book cover than a typical ad.

## Why It Converts
1. **Radical simplicity = zero reading friction for target audience.** Non-native English speakers (the ICP here) can parse this ad in under 1 second.
2. **Lowercase = friendly, not schoolbook.** Correcting English is emotionally fraught. Lowercase headlines say "we're not judging you."
3. **The word "english" is the magnet.** Viewers scanning for language-learning content lock in on the word they care about.
4. **Duo at the bottom is the trust signal.** Mascot recognition = "I know this brand, it's safe to click."
5. **White negative space feels expensive, not lazy.** In a feed full of busy ads, empty space is a pattern interrupt.

## Why It Looks Premium
- Strict minimalism — 3 elements total.
- Type scale controlled (headline > brand, nothing else competes).
- White canvas is the safest possible background but here it's a flex.
- Duo rendered at consistent brand-style proportions.
- Lowercase is a confident stylistic stance, not a typo.

## Creative Director Lens
This is Duolingo's **international localization template.** The same layout runs with "improve your english" / "improve your spanish" / "improve your french" depending on audience — they change one word and relaunch. A CD would recognize this as efficient but not celebrate it — it's functional more than creative. The tonal choice to go lowercase for problem-aware international audiences is the one interesting decision in the ad. Minor critique: the ad is almost *too* quiet — Duo is so small at the bottom that in a Meta feed at thumbnail scale, the mascot is barely visible. Scaling Duo up 30% would maintain the minimalism while increasing stopping power. That said, Duolingo has enough brand recognition that "improve your english" in their signature type is already enough.

## Media Buyer Lens
This is a **localization / international acquisition ad** — almost certainly running to non-English-speaking markets (Latin America, Southeast Asia, Middle East, Eastern Europe). CPM in those markets: $2-8 (much cheaper than US/EU). Expected CTR: 2.0-4.0% (higher because audience intent is high — people who need English learning urgently for jobs/immigration/school). Install rates on this unit will be strong because the copy is maximally accessible. Template performance marketing — Duolingo likely runs 30-50 language variants of this exact layout simultaneously, each targeting a single-language market. Fatigue irrelevant because audiences are geo-segmented and creative is template-driven.

## Transferable Patterns
- **Lowercase, minimal-English headlines for international audiences.** When targeting non-native speakers, reduce vocabulary + formality to match their reading comfort.
- **"Improve your [skill]"** template with one-word swap per audience. "Improve your Spanish" / "Improve your code" / "Improve your fitness" — swap the final word by ICP interest.
- **White canvas + tiny mascot = confident minimalism.** Works only if your brand has existing recognition. Otherwise it reads as an unfinished template.
- **Two-weight single-color palette.** Saturated for headline, muted for supporting — creates hierarchy without size changes.
